Bike Sales

This analysis examines bicycle purchasing patterns based on various demographic and socioeconomic factors, including income, marital status, commuting distance, and age. The project utilized Excel for data processing and created an interactive dashboard that reveals key insights about consumer behavior and bicycle purchasing trends.

Goals

  • Evaluate the relationship between income and bicycle purchases by gender
  • Analyze the impact of commuting distance on purchasing decisions
  • Investigate purchase patterns by age groups

Methodology

  1. Build the data base
  2. Create a Backup
  3. Delete Duplicates
  4. Standardize the data
    • Marital status and gender replacement
    • Age brackets creation using IF formula: =IF(L2>=55;"Old";IF(L2>30;"Middle age";IF(L2 <30;"Young";"Invalid")))
  5. Data quality verification
  6. Pivot tables creation for analysis
  7. Created visualisations

Tools Used

  • Excel: Used for preprocessing and exploratory analysis
  • Pivot Tables: Creation of visualizations and aggregations
  • Dashboard: Interactive interface design with filters

Analysis

Income Analysis by Gender

  • Males with bikes earn 7% more than non-buyers
  • Females with bikes show 4% higher income than non-buyers
  • Average income difference between buyers/non-buyers: $2,624.15
Commuting Patterns
From 1000 total customers:
  • 0-1 Miles: Highest purchase rate (64.6%)
  • 2-5 Miles: Second-highest conversion (23.6%)
  • 10 Miles: Lowest purchase rate (11.8%)
Age Distribution Highlights
  • Middle age: Highest concentration (70.1%)
      • - 54.6% purchase rate
  • Young segment: Smallest group (11%)
      • - 15.1% purchase rate
  • Older segment: Moderate participation (18.9%)
      • - 30.3% purchase rate

Conclusions

The Excel-based analysis revealed compelling insights into bicycle purchasing behavior, demonstrating strong correlations between income levels, commuting distance, and buying decisions. The data clearly shows that customers with higher incomes and shorter commuting distances (0-1 miles) are more likely to purchase bikes, with the middle-age segment driving the majority of sales. These findings provide actionable intelligence for targeted marketing strategies, product development, and market expansion opportunities in high-density, short-commute areas.
